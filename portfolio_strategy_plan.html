AI & automation — how to present it
Dedicated "AI-powered solutions" section — headline ideas & what to show
Section headline (business-facing)
"I build AI into your business — not someday, right now." Show 3–4 practical AI use cases: document analysis, chatbots, auto-reporting, data extraction. Use icons + one-line descriptions.
What to show as AI capabilities
AI-powered dashboards, intelligent search, document summarisation, chatbot integration, workflow automation with LLMs, smart form processing. Frame each as: "Problem it solves → How AI helps."
If you have no AI project yet
Build one small demo app: an AI-powered invoice reader, a customer FAQ chatbot, or an admin dashboard with AI summaries. Even one deployed demo earns the right to claim this capability.
Technologies to mention (for recruiters)
OpenAI / Azure OpenAI API, LangChain or Semantic Kernel (for .NET), Prompt engineering, RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation), REST AI APIs in Next.js and C#.
Hero section — copywriting options

Option B — problem-first
"Your business has problems that software should already be solving. I build the custom tools that fix them — fast, reliable, and built around how you actually work."
Option C — identity-led
"I'm a .NET developer who builds software businesses actually use. Custom web apps, automated workflows, and AI-powered features — all designed around your goals, not just your tech stack."
Layout, feel & design direction
Layout: single-page scroll with anchor navigation
Sticky header with section links. All sections on one page — reduces friction. Smooth scroll. No full-page reloads. Works for all visitor types.
Visual tone: clean dark or deep navy + accent colour
Dark backgrounds signal premium and technical confidence. Pair with one accent (electric blue, teal, or violet). Avoid red/yellow — feels startup-casual, not business-serious.
Above the fold: identity + value + 2 actions
Name, role (not just "Developer" — try "Custom Software & AI Solutions"), one-liner value prop, "View my work" (→ projects) and "Let's talk" (→ contact).
Projects: dual-layer cards
Front of card: business problem + business outcome (for clients). Expand/back: tech stack + architecture decisions (for recruiters). One card, two audiences.
Micro-interactions: subtle animations only
Card hover lifts, section fade-in on scroll, skill icons with hover labels. No autoplay video, no scroll-hijacking. Speed matters — under 2s load on mobile is non-negotiable.
Trust builders: scatter throughout
CS graduate badge, "1+ year .NET experience", GitHub activity graph, live project links, a short testimonial block (even from university or internship), "Available for freelance / hire" status indicator.
What to avoid
Don't open with "Hi, I'm [name] and I'm a passionate developer." Don't list 30 skills as plain text. Don't use stock developer photos. Don't hide contact behind multiple clicks.
How to stand out from traditional portfolios
The "traditional" portfolio trap
Most developer portfolios are: hero ("I'm a passionate dev"), skills list, 3 GitHub projects, contact form. They speak only to recruiters and communicate nothing to business owners or clients. Yours will be different because it leads with problems you solve, not technologies you know.
Problem-led Business outcomes AI-forward Client CTA
Your unique angle: .NET + AI + business thinking
Very few developers in the market position themselves as "I build .NET systems AND I integrate AI into them for business value." That combination — reliable enterprise-grade backend + modern AI capabilities — is a compelling and specific offer that stands out from generic "full-stack developers."
Blog = long-term authority engine
Writing 2–3 posts targeting business owners ("When does your business actually need custom software?", "5 things you can automate with AI this month") builds SEO and positions you as a trusted advisor rather than just a coder for hire. This is what separates premium freelancers from the crowd.
